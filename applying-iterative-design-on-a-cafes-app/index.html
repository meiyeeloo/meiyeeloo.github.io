Applying iterative design on a cafe’s app

Project | Fine Coffee and Flowers
Role | UX research & UI design
Duration | Sep 2021 (1 - 2 weeks)

Introduction

Seeing this decent local cafe had a potential to develop its own app, I have picked it as the topic of my own project to design a new app from scratch.

Problem

The cafe owner needed a comprehensive ordering system with delivery service

Located in Ampang district in Malaysia, Fine Coffee and Flowers is a delicate cafe providing food and beverages with good taste and appearance.

This project aimed to design an exclusive online platform for the cafe to provide food ordering and delivery services, so that they could cut cost of delivery service from their partner.

Research

Kickstart with competitive analysis

Platforms that provided services of cafe ordering and all-kind food delivery were studied. While their experience were not the same, most of them relied on the app as the main platform with optimised features. Their strengths and weaknesses were analysed to seek for any opportunity our competitors had missed.

Trends
  • Optimised app UI
  • Large product image (direct competitors)
Strengths
  • Loyalty program
  • Discounts
  • Provide options for pick up and delivery
Weaknesses
  • Unable to modify the shopping cart (direct competitors)
  • Only 1 out of 4 apps allows in-store order

Based on the research, the design would focus on app which was the main platform utilised in the market. It was surprising that all direct competitors did not allow modfying cart, thus, this part could be done better in design later.

Design & Test

Design interation with usability study

2 rounds of moderated usability study were conducted with users who had ordered at least once a week in order to test the design.

1st iteration – Content had to be user-relevant

In the first round, lo-fi wireframes were prototyped and tested. Although they could complete the ordering, most of them confused about irrelevant information displayed.

2nd iteration – Frictions that disrupted user flow were removed

In this round, hi-fi prototypes were tested. Obvious pauses and hesitation were noticed when user found something confusing or distracting them from completing given tasks.

User flow

I also drew a user flow that showed the complete ordering cycle from a new user to existing user. This gave a clearer visualisation on prototyping the final product.

Final mocks and interactive prototype

Besides major user pain points mentioned above, the fonts were also adjusted in final design to enhance readability as one of my users with presbyopia showed slight difficulty when reviewing order details.

Result

Users satisfied with the intuitive ordering experience

After design iteration, users thought the ordering procedure was simple and straightforward. All of them said they would like to use this app to order in the future.

Next Steps

The next subjects to research and design were listed in order based on their impact on the ordering experience.

  1. Payment details page – to complete the ordering flow as in real life
  2. Account page – for users to customise settings, edit personal details, and review previous orders
  3. Address book page – for users to save more than one delivery address and set the default address

Takeaways

Speaking with the users and understanding the way they think taught me that what I think is useful for the users might not actually be useful for them.

Another thing I learned is conducting usability study with lo-fi wireframes is better when there is a moderater since participants might not understand the context due to the simplified skeleton structure.
